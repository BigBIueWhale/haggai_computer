Make RustDesk Actually Secure

or: how I transmitted my root password across the public internet in cleartext, hundreds of times — and left the line wide enough for a stranger to read my screen and type on it — because a remote-desktop company decided that encrypting the connection was optional, and then locked the bug report.

A design document and public confession by the operator of BigBIueWhale/personal_server, who would very much like their root password back.  ·  2026.  Every code reference below is a pinned, immutable link — to upstream RustDesk at tag 1.4.7 and to the fork at commit 50878a2. Click the receipts.

The short version, for the busy and the burned

RustDesk's “Direct IP Access” mode — the one you use when you want to reach your own machine by its own address without phoning a company's servers — sends everything in plaintext. Your keystrokes (yes, including the sudo password you are typing right now), your screen, your clipboard, your files. And because plaintext means no integrity either, an attacker on your network is not limited to reading it: they can inject their own keystrokes into a session you authenticated and take the machine over — without ever knowing your password. The vendor knows, painted a tiny red icon on it, marked the fix “won't fix,” and locked the bug report. One brave soul forked it to add encryption and got most of it right, but botched the one part that matters — the password handshake is not a real PAKE, so an attacker on your network defeats it on first contact without ever learning your password. The correct fix is a solved problem since the 1990s, ships in your Wi-Fi router, and is roughly 200 lines of code. We are going to do it, make it the only way to connect, and publish builds for every device a normal human owns. Below is exactly the problem, exactly what the fork gets wrong (with line numbers), and exactly the plan.

1. What RustDesk even is, for the uninitiated

RustDesk is open-source remote-desktop software written in Rust with a Flutter user interface. It is the do-it-yourself answer to TeamViewer and AnyDesk: you run one program on the computer you want to control, and the same program — in “viewer” mode — on the phone or laptop in your hand, and you see and drive the far machine's desktop. The controlled machine is launched with a --server flag; despite the name, that is still the ordinary client binary, just sitting still and listening. (There is no second “server program” involved in what I do — hold that thought; it matters in §9.)

There are precisely two ways the viewer can find the controlled machine:

  1. By RustDesk ID, through a rendezvous server. You type a nine-digit number; a coordination server (the company's, at rustdesk.com, or one you host) introduces the two ends and — this is the crucial bit — hands your viewer the controlled machine's signed public key. That key is what every shred of RustDesk's encryption is built on.
  2. By Direct IP Access. You enable a toggle, type the machine's actual IP address, and connect straight to it — no coordination server, no company, no third party, no nine-digit number, nobody's cloud. For anyone who wants to own their own infrastructure end to end, this is obviously the correct choice. It is the choice I made. It is the choice this document is about.

One more fact that will become load-bearing: RustDesk's client is licensed under the AGPLv3 (the GNU Affero General Public License, version 3 — verified in the repository's LICENCE file at tag 1.4.7). That is a strong copyleft license, which means two things simultaneously: (a) anyone may legally fork it, change it, and redistribute the modified program including compiled binaries and Android packages; and (b) they are obligated to publish the corresponding source. In other words: the law has explicitly pre-authorized the fix this document proposes. Keep that in your back pocket for when someone asks “are you allowed to do that?” Yes. Aggressively yes.

2. The Sin: “Direct IP Access” is transmitted in plaintext

The Sin

When you connect by Direct IP Access, RustDesk applies no encryption whatsoever. Not weak encryption. Not outdated encryption. None. The connection carrying your remote desktop is as private as a postcard read aloud.

Here is the mechanism, because I promised to assume nothing is obvious. RustDesk's encryption is bootstrapped from the controlled machine's public key, and — as we just established — that key is delivered to your viewer by the rendezvous server. The encryption design, in upstream's own code, only ever engages when a signed public key has arrived from that server. Direct IP Access deliberately skips the rendezvous server (that is the entire point of it). No server, no delivered key, no key exchange written for the direct path… and so the program simply proceeds in the clear. The relevant logic is a single early-return in src/client.rs line 257: if the peer is a raw IP address, hand back a bare unencrypted socket and never call the secure_connection routine. The controlled side mirrors it: its direct_server listener calls create_tcp_connection with its “secure” flag hardcoded to false, so the entire key-exchange block — guarded by if secure — is skipped.

You do not have to take my word for it. Point a dumb socket at the listening port and read the very first bytes the controlled machine volunteers, before you have said anything:

# the controlled machine, greeting a total stranger, unprompted, in the clear:
HEX  : b04a 2a 0a20 76397763736b68746971676b6b63796d677332336a346365767a6a71667475 12 06 636232783975 ...
ASCII: ·J*· v9wcskhtiqgkkcymgs23j4cevzjqftu2··cb2x9u·
                 └─ the 32-character salt ──────────┘    └ challenge ┘

Those legible strings are the salt and challenge of RustDesk's login handshake, sitting on the wire as readable text. If the channel were encrypted, you would see indistinguishable noise. You see v9wcskhtiqgkkcymgs23j4cevzjqftu2. The password you then type is hashed as SHA-256 of (the hash of password-and-salt, concatenated with the challenge) and sent over that same naked channel — and the entire rest of the session is naked too: your keystrokes travel as KeyEvent messages (so the root password you type into a terminal is on the wire as you type it, blank prompt or no), your screen as VideoFrame messages, plus clipboard and transferred files. Anyone sitting on the path — the café Wi-Fi, the train's network, your internet provider, any one of the autonomous systems your packets transit — reads all of it. There is no decryption step because there was no encryption step. (For the curious: the symmetric session layer that should be wrapping all this is the perfectly good secretbox in hbb_common's tcp.rs — it is sitting right there, unused, because nobody set a key on the direct path.)

The Sin has a second half: a postcard you can also rewrite

Everything above is the confidentiality half — the attacker reads. But “no encryption” is really three holes wearing one coat: no encryption, no integrity, and no authentication of who you are even talking to. Drop the first and the other two come off for free, and the third is the one that upgrades the problem from a stranger is reading my screen to a stranger is typing on my computer.

Here is the mechanism, verified line by line against the 1.4.7 source. The permanent password is checked exactly once. At login the controlled machine issues its challenge, your client answers with the salted hash, the machine compares it in constant time (verify_h1 — a genuine constant-time compare, honest work) and flips a single boolean: self.authorized = true. From that instant on, every frame arriving on that socket is trusted and executed — no second check, no per-message signature, no sequence number, no Poly1305 tag (that lives inside the secretbox nobody keyed). The dispatcher's entire question, for the rest of the session, is the one-liner else if self.authorized. Authorization is a property of the wire, established once — never a property of any individual message.

So put the active attacker back on the café network — the one transparently relaying your connection, the one without your password. They let your login pass through untouched (they cannot forge your hash; they do not need to — they relay it). The instant your machine flips that authorized bit, the attacker begins appending their own frames to the same stream: a KeyEvent here, a MouseEvent there, a Clipboard payload to paste from. The controlled machine cannot tell their frames from yours — same socket, same authorized connection — and so it injects every one into the real desktop through the operating system's input layer, as faithfully as if your own fingers had sent it. With access-mode='full' — which my own README cheerfully tells you to set — keyboard, mouse, and clipboard are all granted. All of it lands.

The Sin, part two

An active attacker on your path does not merely read your session — they can inject their own keystrokes and mouse movements into it and drive your computer, riding the very session you authenticated, without ever knowing the password. The password proves who may open the door. Nothing — not encryption, not a signature, not a single per-message check — defends who may walk through one already open.

Sit with what that means on a box like mine. They are not poking at one stray text box — they are driving my entire live graphical desktop session: the same mouse, keyboard, and clipboard I use, aimed at every window I have open — the file manager, the web browser, the settings panel, a terminal if they fancy one. Opening that terminal and typing a few lines — pasting from the clipboard they also control — is merely the fastest route to add a user, drop an SSH key into authorized_keys, or install a service. The attacker rode one café-Wi-Fi session for ten seconds and walked away with permanent root on a DMZ-exposed machine with an expensive GPU and my sudo rights — long after I shut the laptop. A ten-second eavesdrop becomes a forever tenant.

One honest qualification, because we are not the people who painted the icon: I audited this against the 1.4.7 source, and the single capability the attacker cannot reach this way is RustDesk's graphical file-transfer — that runs as a separate connection which must clear the very same password gate again — its handler refuses to act unless this is a file_transfer login, which the attacker cannot open without the password. But notice how little that narrowing buys: an attacker holding the whole desktop does not need RustDesk's file pane to take your files — they can open the file manager, upload through the browser, or scp from a terminal, because for the length of the session they simply are you at the keyboard. The restriction is real and we will not pretend otherwise; it is also cold comfort.

3. The vendor knew, painted a tiny icon on it, and shrugged

The most galling part is not that this is a bug. It is that it is not a bug — it is a decision, made and defended. RustDesk's own documentation, when you ask about using Direct IP Access, says, and I quote with the typo preserved for the historical record:

“the connection is unecrypted, please do not send us issue about this.”— RustDesk official FAQ wiki

“Please do not send us issue about this.” There is, of course, an issue about this — number #3714, “Encryption for Direct IP Access on a Local Network.” It was opened in March 2023 and closed about four hours later, labeled wontfix. Then, for two and a half years, a procession of confused users filed in beneath it — “why not add an optional 'encrypt the traffic' option?”, “+1,” “I cannot understand why this is still not implemented” — until, in November 2025, a maintainer replied that you should wrap the whole thing in a VPN (“the VPN tunnel already provides end-to-end encryption, so additional encryption within RustDesk is unnecessary”) and locked the thread, so the question could be retired rather than answered. The remedy on offer: go acquire and correctly operate an entirely separate piece of security infrastructure to compensate for the one this remote-desktop tool declined to include.

Let us be clear about the size of what they declined to build. Authenticated key exchange — two parties establishing a shared secret that an eavesdropper cannot derive — was published by Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman in 1976. Doing it such that a password alone proves identity, with no certificates, has been a deployed, standardized primitive since the late 1990s. RustDesk ships, in the year 2026, a remote-desktop product whose flagship serverless mode transmits your administrator's every keystroke — the sudo password included — in cleartext, and the mitigation they offer is a small red shield icon and the words “Insecure Connection” that they treat as informed consent. A shield and a label. As though a tiny graphic is an adequate substitute for the TLS that every login form on the planet has had for fifteen years.

RustDesk's official position

It is unencrypted. They know. They will not fix it. Please stop asking. Have you considered installing a second product to fix our product? Also, here is a red icon.

Here is what I find genuinely strange. RustDesk encrypts every connection that is brokered by a rendezvous server — including one you host yourself, for free, with no account and no telemetry, on your own hardware; the encryption is bootstrapped from the host's signed public key, which that server hands to your viewer. The one mode left naked is the one that asks no server at all — theirs or yours — for permission: you type an address and connect. So it is not that encryption is walled off behind a paid tier; it is that the single serverless, ask-nobody path is the single unencrypted one — and the official remedy for that gap is to go run another server (a VPN). I am not alleging malice; I am noting that “just run a server” is a strange thing to demand of the people who chose the serverless mode precisely so they would not have to, and that the practical consequence — a root session's worth of keystrokes, in cleartext, on a train — is the same whatever the motive. This document shuns the RustDesk company. We will keep their excellent Rust-and-Flutter remote-desktop engine, which is genuinely good work, and we will remove their cryptographic judgment from the building.

4. Exhibit A: my own paranoid server, cheerfully bamboozled

Here is the embarrassing part, offered freely so that you may learn from it instead of repeating it. I run a single-user personal server — an Ubuntu 24.04 LTS box with an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 5090 graphics card — that is documented and scripted to a frankly obsessive standard: every package version pinned to the byte, every install script idempotent and fail-loud, a network-security audit that refuses to let an unexpected port exist. The whole thing is public:

  • The setup guide: github.com/BigBIueWhale/personal_server — README.md
  • The RustDesk install script: github.com/BigBIueWhale/personal_server — scripts/11_install_rustdesk.sh

Read section 14 of that README. It carefully pins RustDesk to version 1.4.6 by exact SHA-256 digest, walks you through enabling Direct IP Access, and then — in the manual hardening steps — instructs you, in earnest:

“Set a permanent password for unattended access… Pick something strong; the box is DMZ-exposed, this password and the SSH password are the two things gating any inbound session.”— my own personal_server README, section 14, written in good faith

Reader, here is the subtlety it took me embarrassingly long to see — and that a tiny red icon does nothing to convey. The permanent password does gate the door. It is a genuine challenge-response: the machine issues a fresh random challenge every connection, your client answers with a salted, challenged hash, and the machine checks it in constant time. So a stranger who merely scans my address cannot walk in, replaying an old login does not work, and a strong password resists guessing. That sentence in my README was not a lie. The trouble is that the gate guards a room with glass walls. Once a session is open the entire thing is cleartext — so every keystroke I typed inside it, including my host's sudo password punched into a terminal, sailed across the open internet in the clear for anyone on the path to read. And the gate is flimsier than it looks: the login password is not sent in the clear, but it is hashed with a fast function and no key-stretching, so a weak one can be lifted off the wire and cracked offline; and an active attacker on my path can hijack the live session and inject their own keystrokes without ever knowing the password at all, because nothing encrypts or signs the stream once the handshake is done. So yes — I version-pinned the SHA-256 digest of a binary whose headline feature was that it had no encryption. I built a vault door; it even worked; and then I bolted it to a tent and did my most sensitive typing inside. (The tent cannot be firewalled either, because RustDesk's peer-to-peer mode sprays a dynamic range of UDP ports — a second gift from the same company.) This is what being bamboozled looks like when you are trying to be careful, and it is precisely why I am writing this down for everyone rather than quietly fixing my own box: if it fooled that setup, it is fooling thousands of people who never even looked.

5. The fork that almost saves us

It turns out someone refused to accept “won't fix.” A developer using the handle ssh4net (Vlad Erium) forked RustDesk into RustAdministrator/rustadmin and actually implemented encryption for the direct path, submitting it upstream as pull request #14674 (where it will, per the policy above, die unmerged). I had it independently audited line by line. Credit where due — the man got a genuinely large amount right, on exactly the philosophy I hold:

  • It reuses RustDesk's existing, proven libsodium cryptography correctly rather than inventing primitives: the XSalsa20-Poly1305 cipher for the session, Curve25519 for key transport, Ed25519 for identity.
  • Session keys are ephemeral per connection, giving forward secrecy.
  • It is fail-closed: the secure handshake is a mandatory step that aborts the connection on any failure, with no silent fallback to plaintext.
  • It re-pins the peer's signing key in the SSH “trust on first use, then warn on change” style, and detects and refuses a changed identity.
  • It is sovereign by design: a “Local Only” mode, an explicit refusal to honor relay hosts handed out by any rendezvous server, the deliberate removal of implicit fallbacks to the company's infrastructure.

So the architecture is right, the philosophy is mine, and the unglamorous plumbing is competent. The good news is real: someone has shown the engine can be made to do the right thing. And then there is the one part that is wrong, and it is wrong in the single most important place.

6. Exactly what the fork gets wrong (it's the whole ballgame)

The fork offers an optional pairing passphrase as the way to establish trust on the very first connection without pre-sharing keys — exactly the feature a sane person wants, because it is the only thing that lets you connect from a borrowed device with nothing pre-installed. The intent is perfect. The execution is not a PAKE. It is a one-directional party trick, and here is the precise shape of it — every claim is a link to the exact line in the fork at commit 50878a2:

  • The viewer computes a proof of the passphrase — roughly the SHA-256 of (a domain tag, the Argon2id-hardened passphrase, the peer identity, and the keys) — and sends it to the controlled machine.
  • The controlled machine checks it. Fine so far.
  • But the controlled machine's “success, you may trust me” reply is a fixed, hardcoded constant: the literal bytes b"direct-ok", sent on success. It proves nothing. It is a participation trophy.

Do you see it? Only one side ever proves knowledge of the password. Now watch a person-in-the-middle on your network — the café, the train, your hostile internet provider — defeat it on first contact, without ever learning your passphrase:

1. You connect. The attacker sits in the middle and presents their own keys,
   claiming to be your machine. You have no pin yet — it's first contact — so nothing rejects this.
2. Your viewer seals the session key to the attacker's key and computes the passphrase proof.
3. You type your real passphrase. Your viewer sends the proof... to the attacker.
4. The attacker doesn't bother verifying it. It just echoes the magic constant: b"direct-ok".
5. Your viewer accepts the constant and pins the attacker's key as your machine — forever.
   You are now fully person-in-the-middled. The attacker never learned the passphrase. It didn't need to.

That fatal step 5 — the viewer accepting b"direct-ok" as proof of identity — is the entire vulnerability in one line. The passphrase, as built, stops a passive eavesdropper (who was already stopped by the encryption) and does precisely nothing against the active attacker the entire feature exists to defeat. And the active attacker it waves through is no spectator: having pinned itself as your machine, it now wields exactly the keystroke-injection power of §2 — driving your desktop, owning the box — only now wrapped in a channel your client has been fooled into trusting. (As a bonus indignity, the proof the attacker collects is offline-guessable, slowed only by Argon2id; and the proof comparison is a plain == rather than the constant-time check the fork uses for the password — the kind of thing that becomes a timing oracle after one innocent refactor.) And this is no stale-commit artifact. The constant-ack design predates the fork's branch split — it was introduced before main and the separate Security branch (the one the upstream pull request was opened from) diverged — so it sits identically on both, and none of the later commits on either branch remove it. The fork's whole answer to first-contact trust is more fingerprint-comparison and key-pinning ceremony — the very manual ritual we are trying to abolish — layered on top of the constant b"direct-ok", never in place of it. No actual PAKE primitive is imported anywhere in the codebase, on either branch.

In one sentence

The fork is not “almost a PAKE,” in the same way a parachute that opens almost every time is not a parachute. It is the right airplane with the wrong parachute, and we are going to pack the right one.

7. The grown-up answer, which is embarrassingly well-known

What we want is not exotic. It has a name, a standards body, and shipping deployments measured in the billions. It is a Password-Authenticated Key Exchange — a PAKE, to its friends. A balanced PAKE lets two parties who share one secret (a password) establish a mutually-authenticated, encrypted channel with the following properties, all at once:

  • No public-key infrastructure. No certificates, no pinned keys, no fingerprints to eyeball. Just the password.
  • Mutually authenticating. The channel is provably between two parties who both know the password. Since I am the only one who knows the password to my own machine, “both parties know the password” means “me, and my machine.” That is the entire requirement you articulated, formalized.
  • Person-in-the-middle proof. An attacker without the password cannot complete the exchange with either side. The most they can do is relay ciphertext they cannot read — i.e., be a glorified network cable.
  • Offline-attack proof. The wire transcript reveals nothing brute-forceable. The attacker is reduced to one online guess per connection attempt — which, against a long random password and any rate-limiting at all, is a rounding error away from zero.

You already trust a PAKE with your home. WPA3 uses one — the SAE handshake (a.k.a. Dragonfly) — and it is literally the fix for the “capture the WPA2 handshake and crack it offline” attack that haunted the previous generation. The magic-wormhole file-transfer tool uses SPAKE2 for exactly the “two machines, one short shared phrase, no servers, no eavesdropping” pattern. The IETF's Crypto Forum Research Group ran a multi-year selection process and, on 20 March 2020, formally recommended two: CPace (balanced) and OPAQUE (augmented). This is not a research frontier. This is a paved road with streetlights. RustDesk drove past it to ship a postcard.

8. Our plan, cryptographically

We fork RustDesk 1.4.7 (and its protocol submodule hbb_common), and we replace the broken pairing trinket with a real balanced PAKE. The shape of the change is small and surgical, because — credit again to the fork — the message envelope is already the right shape:

  • Primitive: a balanced PAKE. The fastest correct path is SPAKE2 via the RustCrypto spake2 crate (pure Rust, Curve25519, interoperable with magic-wormhole). If we prefer the Crypto Forum Research Group's balanced selection, CPace. Either gives us §7 in full.
  • Swap the proof for an exchange. The one-directional compute_direct_pairing_proof becomes the PAKE's start/finish messages, carried in the existing handshake envelope.
  • Kill the participation trophy. The controlled machine's reply stops being the constant b"direct-ok" and becomes a key-confirmation Message Authentication Code, bound to the full transcript (both ephemeral Curve25519 keys, the signing key, the peer identity) — which the viewer verifies before trusting anything. This single change is what makes first contact mutual, and it is the line that closes the entire attack in §6.
  • Derive the session key from the PAKE output, so it is no longer sealed to an attacker-chosen key before authentication — eliminating the “seal to the person-in-the-middle” race.
  • Constant-time everything that touches a secret, and rate-limit online guesses.

The auditor's estimate, which matches mine, is on the order of 150–300 lines across src/client.rs, src/server.rs, and src/common.rs plus the protocol submodule, plus one line in Cargo.toml. Two hundred lines. That is the size of the thing a venture-backed company looked at and said “please do not send us issue about this.”

One handshake, five doors — and a trap door

Those two hundred lines buy the handshake; spending them well means knowing every door they have to lock — and RustDesk has more doors than it advertises. It does not carry a single kind of session, it multiplexes five of them over the same connection, chosen by one field in the login message: ordinary remote-control, file transfer, port-forwarding (which is also how an RDP session is tunneled), view-camera (a live camera feed), and a first-class remote terminal (a shell — no need to touch the desktop at all). The reassuring half is that all five are dealt the same hand: one TCP stream, through one choke point, behind one password gate — and the message that picks the kind is read only after the handshake. Key that one stream with the PAKE before the login is parsed and all five doors lock at once. We do not write the fix five times; we write it where the stream is born.

The other half is a trap door, and it is literal. When RustDesk tunnels a port — most consequentially an RDP session to the desktop's own localhost:3389 — it calls, on both ends, set_raw() on the very stream it just secured — which throws the encryption key away and drops the tunnel to a bare pipe. This is not a flaw we are introducing; it is already true of upstream's encrypted mode, which is precisely why a forwarded RDP or database session crosses the wire in the clear even when the rest of RustDesk is wrapped. A fix that only keys the handshake would look finished and still leak the single highest-value tunnel on the machine.

The trap door

“We added encryption” is the easiest lie to tell yourself here: key the handshake, watch the desktop session turn to noise on the wire, ship — and never notice that every port-forwarded RDP session still travels in plaintext, because the tunnel quietly threw the key away. Full security means three things: all five session kinds covered by that one handshake; the port-forward key-strip sealed shut — keep the key on the tunnel, or refuse to forward at all; and the message loop itself gated on the handshake, so that every path the protocol uses to grant a session — the password login, the 30-second reconnect window, the session hand-off — is processed only on a stream the PAKE has already keyed and mutually authenticated, never on a bare one. We count every door, not only the front one.

The crucial part — non-negotiable

In our fork, the secure PAKE handshake is the only way to connect. We do not add it as a setting. We do not make it a default-on toggle that a future menu could flip off. We delete the plaintext direct path, delete the allow-unverified-peer-trust escape hatch, and delete the fingerprint-comparison ceremony. There is no insecure fallback to misconfigure, no downgrade to negotiate, no red icon to ignore. The connection is mutually password-authenticated and encrypted, or it does not happen. A tool that can be configured into insecurity will be, by someone, on the worst possible day. Ours cannot be.

An honest caveat, because we are not RustDesk

The available Rust PAKE crates are themselves not yet independently audited. So this plan trades one unaudited cryptographic implementation for a principled, standardized one — strictly better, but still warranting a real cryptographer's review of the key-confirmation construction, the transcript binding, the constant-time comparisons, and the key-derivation parameters before anyone stakes their root account on it across the open internet. We will say so loudly, every time, which is the entire difference between us and the people who painted the icon.

9. How I actually use this thing, and every application we must patch

This is a tool for a real life, not a benchmark. Concretely, I rely on the full feature set, and the fix must preserve all of it:

  • A complete graphical desktop of the controlled machine, driven in real time.
  • File transfer through the graphical interface — drag files between machines, not a separate ceremony.
  • Session persistence — I reconnect to the same live desktop, applications still open, exactly where I left it.
  • Parallel connections — viewing and controlling one machine from my phone and my computer simultaneously.
  • Multiple machines — I also drive a second Ubuntu server on my local network the same way.
  • From anywhere, by address — from home, or from a train, on whatever device is in my hand.

Now, the part you specifically need to get right when you fork this, and the part that confuses everyone: which programs, and which repositories? RustDesk is one cross-platform codebase, not a separate app per device. That single codebase is compiled into every one of these client applications, all of which we must patch (by patching the one shared codebase) and then build and publish:

So, to your worry about “other repositories”: the answer is mostly reassuring.

  • The one repository to fork is github.com/rustdesk/rustdesk — and you must also fork its protocol submodule github.com/rustdesk/hbb_common, because the handshake code we are rewriting lives across both (the client/server logic in the main repository, the wire protocol and transport in the submodule). That is the whole source.
  • There is no separate “server” program to fork. The controlled side is the same client binary in --server mode. The thing that is a separate repository — RustDesk's rendezvous-and-relay server pair, hbbs/hbbr, in rustdesk/rustdesk-server — we deliberately do not use. In fairness, self-hosting that pair is the sanctioned road to a setup that is sovereign and encrypted: every rendezvous-brokered connection, including one served by your own free hbbs, gets the real host-authenticated, end-to-end encryption that Direct IP lacks. We pass on it for concrete reasons, not dogma — it adds several internet-facing ports to a box whose whole discipline is one audited port; it means running and key-managing two more services; and it surrenders the one property this fork exists to protect: that you reach the machine by typing its address, from any device, with nothing to pre-configure. The PAKE fork buys the encryption without any of that. (My personal_server README, section 14, labors over this exact “three things called RustDesk” confusion.)
  • Every platform builds from that one forked repository via its GitHub Actions continuous-integration workflows (the Flutter build pipeline produces the Android, Windows, and Linux artifacts). The one genuinely open task here is operational, not architectural: make the release pipeline actually run and publish — the existing fork's automation ran essentially once and emitted nothing, which is precisely why it has “no .apk, no installer, build it yourself.” We must wire the Flutter SDK, the Android NDK, and the Windows and Debian toolchains into a release that produces signed, downloadable binaries for every row of that table. Verify each platform's workflow as you go; assume nothing builds until you have watched it build.

10. What we ship, and a benediction

The deliverable is the thing the world should have had all along and that a remote-desktop company specifically chose not to provide: a fork of RustDesk's perfectly good engine in which connecting by your own machine's address is mutually password-authenticated and encrypted, by the only path the program offers, with a real PAKE doing the work — and a GitHub releases page bearing a downloadable application for Android smartphones, for Microsoft Windows 10 and Microsoft Windows 11, and for Debian and Ubuntu GNU/Linux in both viewer and controlled roles — with full corresponding source, because the AGPLv3 obligates it and because we are, unlike some, not ashamed of our code.

It will take roughly two hundred lines and one honest sentence about wanting a cryptographer to check our homework. It is, in the most literal and damning sense, that simple. It was always that simple. Someone just had to decide that your administrator password deserved more protection than a small red icon.

We are deciding that. Shun the vendor; keep the engine; pack the right parachute.
